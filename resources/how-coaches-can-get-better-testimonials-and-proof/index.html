Strong coaches still need visible proof.

A coach can be excellent and still look unproven online. If your clients get results but your testimonials are vague, your case studies are thin, or your outcomes live only in memory, prospects have to take too much on faith.

By Mitch Russo · Updated June 18, 2026

Expertise is not the same thing as evidence.

Many coaches are better than their marketing makes them look. They know how to create insight, accountability, and change, but they do not capture the evidence while the work is happening.

That leads to testimonials like "great coach" or "helped me a lot." Those are kind, but they do not give a future buyer enough to believe.

Better proof is created during the engagement, not after it.

The worst time to build a case study is after the client has already forgotten the journey. By then, the starting point is fuzzy, the small wins are gone, and the coach is trying to reconstruct proof from scattered notes.

Better testimonials come from a better record: what the client wanted, what changed, what they completed, what improved, and what they can now do differently.

What this coach needs first

  • Session notes that capture decisions and milestones.
  • Homework and accountability records that show client action.
  • Progress charts tied to the client's stated goals.
  • Reports that make transformation easier to summarize.
  • A repeatable way to turn completed work into testimonials and case studies.

The Under-Proven Expert FAQs

Why are many coaching testimonials too weak?

They are usually collected too late and without enough context. Strong testimonials are easier when the coach can point to goals, assignments, milestones, and measurable change.

What is progress proof for coaches?

Progress proof is structured evidence that a client is learning, acting, and improving. It can include completed homework, session milestones, ratings, reports, and before-and-after examples.
